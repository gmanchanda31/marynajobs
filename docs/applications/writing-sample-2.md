# Writing Sample 2 — Plain-English explanation of a technical concept

**Prompt context:** Annotation platforms also test "explainer" writing — taking a niche concept and rendering it for a non-expert reader. This sample uses a workforce-management term Maryna actually owned daily at Konecta.

**Prompt:**

> Explain in plain English what "shrinkage" means in a customer-service operation, for someone who has never worked in operations. Keep it under 250 words and avoid jargon where possible.

---

## Response

If you run a customer-service team of, say, 100 people, you might assume that every one of those people is on the phones for every minute of their shift. They're not — and that's where "shrinkage" comes in.

Shrinkage is the percentage of paid work time that *isn't* spent doing the actual job. It includes things you'd expect, like coffee breaks and lunch, and things you might not, like training sessions, team meetings, the few minutes between calls when an agent is writing notes, sick leave, or the moment someone steps away from their desk to hand a delivery driver a package.

In a healthy operation, shrinkage usually sits somewhere around 25 to 35 percent. That sounds high until you realise it covers everything from a fifteen-minute coaching huddle on Tuesday morning to the holiday someone takes in August.

Why anyone outside of operations should care: shrinkage is the single biggest reason a team that "looks" fully staffed on paper still misses its service level. If you've planned for 100 agents but forgot that 30 percent of their time is shrinkage, you're really only getting 70 working agents. Forecasting it accurately is half the battle.

The good news is that shrinkage isn't bad — most of it is normal, healthy, and necessary. The job is just to *measure* it honestly so you can plan around it.

---

## Why this works as an annotation sample

- **Concrete framing** — opens with a vivid image (100-person team).
- **Definition before details** — gives the reader the answer up front.
- **Honest range, not a single number** — shows nuance, not memorised facts.
- **Real-world examples** — coffee, training, holidays — not abstract categories.
- **Soft conclusion** — reframes shrinkage as a planning input, not a problem to eliminate.
- **Demonstrates domain experience** — Maryna isn't just writing about WFM, she ran it.

Good fit for "explain X in plain English" prompts on DataAnnotation, Outlier, and Surge.
